About

Bridging astrophysics, statistics, and machine learning

I am a dual Ph.D. candidate in Astrophysics and Astrobiology at the University of Washington, working at the intersection of astronomy, statistics, and artificial intelligence.

My research turns massive astronomical datasets into physical insight — applying machine learning, Bayesian inference, and large-scale simulation to questions ranging from the nature of dark matter to the dynamics of small Solar System bodies. Alongside the science, I build the high-performance computing pipelines that make this analysis possible at survey scale.

I earned my dual B.S. in Astrophysics and Geophysics from UCLA with Departmental Highest Honors, and I am completing a dual M.S. in Astrophysics and Statistics alongside my doctoral work. Beyond research, I have helped lead student scientific communities — co-founding the Data Science Society at UW and serving as President of UCLA's Astronomical Society — and I am an active astrophotographer drawn to the same skies I study.

Research

Selected research

Three threads of my doctoral work — the small bodies of the Solar System, the nature of dark matter, and the time-domain sky — each built on large datasets and scientific computing.

01

Temporary Jovian Co-orbitals

Active

2025 – Present  ·  Advisor: Prof. Sarah Greenstreet

A high-performance pipeline that integrates asteroid orbits to identify objects temporarily captured into co-orbital resonance with Jupiter. For each of 15,000+ asteroids, it generates 1,000 statistical clones to propagate observational uncertainty, then runs N-body simulations across millions of years to trace long-term dynamical evolution. Classification algorithms flag anomalous trajectories, enabling robust detection of these short-lived co-orbital states.

02

Inferring Dark Matter Subhalos

Recent

2024 – 2025  ·  Advisor: Prof. Nora Shipp

Simulation-based inference for the dark matter subhalos that perturb stellar streams. Using Neural Posterior Estimation, the method recovers a perturber's mass, scale radius, velocity, impact parameter, and orientation directly from stream data. Transformer-based networks with conditional normalizing flows — trained on 150,000+ particle-spray simulations — yield well-calibrated posteriors across a high-dimensional parameter space, and forecast how deeper surveys will sharpen these constraints.

03

Rotation Periods of Jupiter Trojans

Completed

2022 – 2024  ·  Advisor: Prof. Željko Ivezić

A time-domain study of Jupiter Trojan asteroids using ZTF light curves. The work processes millions of noisy time-series observations through statistical filtering and noise-correction pipelines, then applies period-finding algorithms with cross-validation against external catalogs to recover reliable rotation periods. The resulting population analysis identifies a spin-barrier limit of 4–4.8 hours and constrains the physical structure of these primitive bodies.

Research interests & directions

Rubin Observatory & LSST

Preparing analysis methods for the Legacy Survey of Space and Time — the decade-long, billions-of-objects time-domain survey from the Vera C. Rubin Observatory — building on time-series work with precursor surveys.

SETI & Technosignatures

The search for technosignatures — applying anomaly detection and large-scale signal processing to identify the rare, the unexplained, and the potentially artificial in astronomical data.

Exoplanets & Radial Velocity

Detecting and characterizing exoplanets from radial-velocity and photometric time series, where careful statistical modeling separates faint planetary signals from stellar and instrumental noise.

Astronomy AI at Scale

Machine learning purpose-built for astronomy — simulation-based inference, deep learning, and anomaly detection that turn survey-scale datasets into physical understanding.
